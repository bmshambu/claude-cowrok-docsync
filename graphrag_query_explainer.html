What's in this folder

100 procurement RFPs were ingested, covering 20 industry sectors (Healthcare, Financial Services, Energy, Retail, Aerospace…) and 10 service lines (Enterprise Software Platform, Cloud Migration, ERP Implementation, Managed Security, Data Warehouse Modernization…). Instead of storing them as plain text, DocSync read every page and built a knowledge graph — a map of the buyers, the services they want, their indicative budgets, and their strategic drivers, plus how all of those connect. The tabs below show three kinds of question you can ask it, and the "vs Traditional RAG" tab shows why a graph answers questions that ordinary search cannot.

SIMPLE LOOKUP

One specific fact

Finds one entity, then follows its direct links (1–2 hops). Best when you know which RFP or service you're asking about.

"What's the budget for the ERP project at Energy Holdings?"

ACROSS DOCUMENTS

Patterns across the corpus

Reads cluster summaries first, then pulls supporting chunks. Best for "which RFPs…", "compare across…", or trend questions.

"Compare strategic drivers across the cloud and ERP RFPs"

CONNECTED

Specific + thematic

Combines both paths — finds specific entities (e.g. budgets) and also groups them thematically. Used when a question is both a filter and a pattern.

"Which RFPs have budgets over USD 2M, and what are they for?"

The 3-Layer Architecture Your Queries Run Through

Two ways to answer a question from documents

Most AI document tools use traditional RAG: they chop documents into chunks, and when you ask a question they fetch the chunks whose wording looks most similar to your question. That works well when the answer sits in one passage. It struggles when the answer has to be counted, filtered, or assembled across many documents. A knowledge graph stores the facts and their connections, so it can filter and follow them.

TRADITIONAL RAG

Finds similar text

Turns every chunk into a vector of numbers and returns the chunks that look most like your question. It never "knows" that an RFP requires a service or has a budget — it only sees words that look alike.

Good at: "Summarise the scope of one RFP."
Weak at: "How many RFPs…", "Which RFPs…", or anything spanning all 100 files.

GRAPHRAG (this system)

Follows relationships

Stores each buyer, service, budget and strategic driver as linked facts. To answer, it filters and walks those links — so "every budget above USD 2M" is a query, not a guess.

Good at: counting, filtering, comparing and connecting across the whole corpus, with exact page citations.

Simple question both can handle: "What's the budget for the ERP project at Energy Holdings?"
R
Traditional RAG: searches for chunks similar to "ERP budget Energy" → finds the budget line in RFP-006 → returns it. Works, because the answer lives in one passage.
G
GraphRAG: finds Energy Holdings, follows requires to the ERP Implementation service, then has_budget to the figure — USD 1,775,134–2,817,111, citing RFP-006 page 1.
Verdict: A tie for single-fact questions. The difference shows up below.
Counting question that trips up traditional RAG: "Which RFPs have an indicative budget over USD 2M?"
R
Traditional RAG: can only return a handful of chunks that mention large numbers. It cannot reliably scan all 100 budgets or compare them numerically — so it under-counts and misses RFPs whose budget wording doesn't resemble the question.
G
GraphRAG: every budget is a node with a numeric max. It filters them in one pass — 23 RFPs can exceed USD 2M, of which 3 sit entirely above it (RFP-051, RFP-050, RFP-031).
Why it matters: "How many" and "which ones" are filter-and-count questions. A graph answers them exactly; similarity search can only approximate from whatever chunks it happened to retrieve.
Cross-document comparison: "Compare strategic drivers across the cloud and ERP RFPs"
R
Traditional RAG: retrieves a few "objectives" chunks. If most top hits come from cloud RFPs, the ERP side is under-represented and the comparison is lopsided — it never sees the full set.
G
GraphRAG: each driver is a shared concept node linking back to every RFP that names it. The pattern falls out cleanly — cloud RFPs cluster on growth/agility, ERP RFPs on risk/cost/control.
Cloud drivers: operational efficiency · faster time-to-market · customer & employee experience
ERP drivers: reduce technology risk & technical debt · lower total cost of ownership · compliance & audit readiness
Shared by both: process standardization · data visibility for leadership

1
Lookup path: filter all budget nodes where max ≥ 2,000,000 → 23 RFPs qualify; 3 sit entirely above USD 2M
2
Graph traversal: for each budget, hop back has_budget → service, then requires → client, to name what each spend is for
3
Across-docs path: the high-value cluster concentrates in three service lines — Enterprise Software Platform, Application Support, and ERP Implementation
4
Merged answer: the filtered list, each tied to its service and sector, with page citations
Top 3 (entire range above USD 2M):
RFP-051 — Enterprise Software Platform, Hospitality — USD 2.16M–3.39M
RFP-050 — Application Support Services, Insurance — USD 2.16M–2.78M
RFP-031 — Enterprise Software Platform, Hospitality — USD 2.13M–2.68M

Why hybrid: "over USD 2M" is a filter (lookup path); "what are they for" is a grouping (across-docs path). One question, both engines.
